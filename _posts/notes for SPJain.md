## Derek Abells 7C's of corporate Governance,

1. Code of conduct: Define clear rules for accepted behaviour within your company.
2. Communication:   Ensure that everybody in your organisation/ team knows the rules, talk about the control mechanisms that you employ, and how you will address inacceptable behaviour.
3. Control mechanisms: Establish control mechanisms to ensure compliance with your defined rules.
4. Compensation and incentives: Set the priorities of your people right by linking compensation and benefits to the adherence to your code of conduct.
5. Censure: Clearly address misbehaviour; consider addressing it in public.
6. Culture: Establish a culture within your organisation/ team that will take ethical aspects of business serious
7. Personal leadership conduct and conviction: Serve as a role model.

## Deontological

## Utilitarian theory 
This theory suggests that we find the meaning of ethics by looking at the consequences. In particular, something is right if it maximises the good, producing the greatest good for the greatest number.
The stress in all this is the maximisation of social goods and, in particular, happiness. The bankers’ defence relies heavily on this theory. The utilitarian approach argues that economic growth and wealth are key to the happiness and well-being of all.

- act utilitarianism, focusing on the act that is likely to produce the most good. 
- rule utilitarianism, focusing on sets of rules or codes that would produce the most good. The bankers’ argument seems to fit into this category. As a rule, the argument goes, it is right to pay big bonuses because of their effect on the finance system and the wider happiness of society.

But what are the criteria for deciding what a higher pleasure is, and why it is good? In any case, who decides? 
The theory does not help in actually defining what is good. A higher pleasure might be defined by some as high culture, by others as survival, and by yet others as psychological well-being. 
Any definition of happiness or the good may well depend on different cultural views – and how do you decide between them?
The stress on consequences can easily lead to the end justifying any means. Can it be right to kill the populations of two entire cities in order to end World War II? Can it be right to accept or give bribes to keep a workforce employed? 

The stress on the good for the majority can easily lead to the oppression of the minority. Just because something will benefit 50.5% of the population does not make it good and might in any case disadvantage the 49.5%.

## Promote a cause - What 
They have been a common form of corporate giving, along with philanthropy and employee volunteerism.
In this, a company provides funds, in-kind contribution or other corporate resources to increase awareness and concern about a social cause or to support fundraising, participation or volunteer recruitment for a cause.
Persuasive communication is the major focus of this initiative.
Such campaign plans are based on clear definition of target audiences, communication objectives and goals.

## Promote a cause - How 
Motivating through presentation  of statistics and facts – Hungry/ homeless people.
Persuading people to find out more about the cause by visiting a special website
Persuading people to donate their time – help to senior citizens.
Persuading people to donate non-monetary resources- used spectacles, computers.
Persuading people to participate in events- attending a fund-raising marathon, signing a petition to ban animal testing. Body Shop

## Promote a cause - When 
When a company has easy access to the target markets. 
When the cause can be connected and sustained by a company’s products.
When the opportunity exists to contribute underutilized in-kind services, such as in-house expertise.
When employee involvement will support the cause.
When a company wants to limit its involvement and commitment to raising awareness and concern versus the attempt to change the target audience’s behaviour.
When there is co-branding opportunity.
