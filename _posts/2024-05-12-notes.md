## Transformers - transforms an input sequence to an output sequence
- Transformers were first developed to solve the problem of sequence transduction, or 
- neural machine translation, 
- which means they are meant to solve any task that transforms an input sequence to an output sequence. 

A transformer model is a neural network 
that learns the context of sequential data and generates new data out of it.

A transformer is a type of artificial intelligence model that learns to understand and generate human-like text by analyzing patterns in large amounts of text data.

Transformers are a current state-of-the-art NLP model and 
are considered the evolution of the encoder-decoder architecture. 
While the encoder-decoder architecture relies mainly on Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) to extract sequential information, Transformers completely lack this recurrency.

They are specifically designed to comprehend context and 
meaning by analyzing the relationship between different elements, and 
they rely almost entirely on a mathematical technique called attention to do so.

## Transactional database - ACID 

- Atomicity
- Either Do or Dont do. There is no try. 
- Atomicity requires that every step in a transaction be completed in its entirety before being committed to the database. 
- If there’s a failure at any stage of the transaction, the other steps will also fail or be reverted. 
- 
- Consistency
- Once done, is done. 
- The transaction is either successfully written to the database, moving it from one valid state to another, or it is automatically reverted. 
- This enables the database to remain in a constant state of internal consistency.
- 
- Isolation
- Mone, bone, kone
- ACID-compliant transactional databases require that individual transactions run independently. 
- Transactions that have not been fully processed are not able to be acted on or modified by other transactions. 
- 
- Durability
- Ek bar commit kar doon to apne aap ka bhi nahin sunta
- When transactions have been fully processed and committed to the database, they cannot be lost, even if the database fails. 

## Big Data 

- Big data refers to extremely large and diverse data (volume)
	- spurred on by digital technology advancements, 
	- such as connectivity, mobility, the Internet of Things (IoT), and artificial intelligence (AI). 
- collections of structured, unstructured, and semi-structured data (variety)
- that continues to grow exponentially over time. (velocity)
- that traditional data management systems cannot store, process, and analyze them. 

### Volume - extremely large amount of data 

### Velocity - refers to the speed at which data is generated. 
- data is often produced in real time or near real time, and 
- it must also be processed, accessed, and analyzed at the same rate to have any meaningful impact. 

### Variety
- Data is heterogeneous, 
- meaning it can come from many different sources and can be structured, unstructured, or semi-structured. 
- More traditional structured data (such as data in spreadsheets or relational databases) 
- unstructured text, images, audio, video files, or 
- semi-structured formats like sensor data 
- that can’t be organized in a fixed data schema. 

### Veracity - Can we trust the data? 
- Big data can be messy, noisy, and error-prone, 
- difficult to control the quality and accuracy of the data. 
- Large datasets can be unwieldy and confusing,
- while smaller datasets could present an incomplete picture. 
- The higher the veracity of the data, the more trustworthy it is.

### Variability
- The meaning of collected data is constantly changing, which can lead to inconsistency over time. 
- These shifts include not only changes in context and interpretation but also 
- data collection methods based on the information that companies want to capture and analyze.

### Value
- It’s essential to determine the business value of the data you collect. 
- Big data must contain the right data and then be effectively analyzed in order to yield insights that can help drive decision-making.

# [Data Democratization in the Era of GenAI](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/data-democratization-era-genai-arnab-chakraborty-czlec/)


Data democratization is the process of making data easily accessible, and discoverable to a wide array of users within an organization. 
In the traditional setup, the responsibility for extracting value from data falls on a select group of individuals, typically Data Analysts or, when dealing with complex predictive tasks or discovering novel patterns, Data Scientists. 

This model doesn’t quite align with the principles of true democracy, where everyone enjoys certain rights. Instead, it resembles more of an autocracy, but with a distinct twist. 

In this scenario, the chosen few have earned their position through extensive knowledge and training rather than any kind of “coup".

- Layers that data goes through in an enterprise
	- Operational DB / Applications 
	- Data warehouse / Lake / Lakehouse
	- Data Nerds - Analysts, Scientists
	- Dashboards, Reports, Chat, ... ai/ml driven apps 
	- Data consumers 

- Choking point in this process is the Data Nerds

Option 1: Hire an army of Data Nerds
Option 2: Find a way to democratize data to all 

The solution is Data Democratization to some extent. 
This particular term is been in the market for quite some time. 
However, very few industries can proudly say that they have achieved a truly democratic data ecosystem where the data can be accessed by anyone(with authorization) with the right tool. 
Why is it so difficult? There are a number of reasons. 

Before I start explaining how I think GenAI/LLM(Large Language Models) is going to solve most of them, 
let’s first understand if the majority is ready to take control of their data. 
If we look into any STEM course we will observe that Data Analysis is a big part of the curriculum. 
It is essential in almost any discipline. 
If you ask any of the students or professionals, you will find that almost all of them are quite well-versed in the concepts. 
They have already done one or the other classical data analysis, 
have an idea about p-value, know what regression is, and can do time series forecasting. 
Many professionals possess the skills through education, training, or self-learning. 
However, in an industrial setting, unless they belong to the select group of data enthusiasts, they are the majority — waiting for the results of the analysis to adapt to the evolving business landscape.

STEM course ??? 

Tools are a big part of the data ecosystem. 
Tools are everywhere from ingestion to visualization. 
And for every aspect, there are too many tools. 
These tools oftentimes lack interoperability. 
Industry-wise, organization and even team-wise the tools vary significantly. 
The selection of the tool is largely tied up with the underlying data infrastructure the particular ecosystem is using. 
Often a specialized data professional struggles to cope with tools. 
Organizations are so married to the tools that their job adverts for data professionals focus primarily on the know-how of the tools. 
In my role, I am also asked this question often which tool should I learn? 
My standard answer is does not really matter. 
Focusing on the underlying concept is what matters. 
Alas, interview questions focus on tool-specific things. 
Any true data professionals know that as long as you know the concept, tools can be learned in days, or weeks and mastered within a few months. 
The obsessions around tools keep the majority of the “non” data professionals away. 

The big fallacy here is the low-code, no-code platforms. 
They are supposed to help to scale the adaptability of the data platforms. 
However in reality these platforms add to the long tail of the never-ending tool list. 
Often “non” data professionals do not want to spend time learning the tools with a fear that it may get obsolete soon. 

And they can utilize their time on other stuff leaving the data tasks to data nerds.

GenAI can be a saviour for such instances. 
Extracting value from the data rarely depends on the proficiency of the tools. 
It is a function of Understanding Data and Asking Questions. 
These two are tool-agnostic. 

I am an advocate of “Full Stack Data Professional”. 
I truly believe that a true data professional has no boundaries. 
It’s us who created Data analysts, Data Scientists, and Data Engineers boundaries and often we make it so difficult to transition from one area to another. 
However, my expectation is that the low-code/no-code tools will be able to break these barriers and help us produce true “Full Stack Data Professionals”.

- 
- These tools are the true enablers of Data Democratization. 
- Almost anyone build a pipeline with the intuitive artefacts that these tools offer. 
- In reality, almost none can build an effective industry-accepted data pipeline with the tools alone. 
- We would need to heavily rely upon custom coding which again creates hindrance for data democratization.
- 
- A GenAI wrapper on top of the tooling platform will do the necessary trick. 
- It would require solid Metadata which will power the build of the data pipeline. 
- The LLM models in the background need to be sufficiently trained to choose between available artefacts vs custom-written codes. 
- The basic fundamental of the pipeline is that it needs to be easy to understand. 
- The model should sufficiently explain the custom codes where it has been used.
- 
- This would encourage more users to come forward and experiment with data. 
- Most importantly it will also encourage non-data professionals to experiment with AI. 
- Whilst most of the heavy lifting will be done by the low-code/no-code platform the remaining coding superpower will be handled by the GenAI wrapper. 
- It can effectively translate an instruction into a code.

# [What Is Data Democratization? Definition and Principles](https://amplitude.com/blog/data-democratization)

- It is NOT 
- Is data democratization about “access to data” only
- data access—whether as raw data in a data warehouse ? 
- beautiful visualizations inside a product analytics tool or a business intelligence tool—is certainly not data democratization.


- Data democratization is the ongoing process of enabling everybody in an organization, 
- irrespective of their technical know-how, 
- to work with data comfortably, 
- to feel confident talking about it, and, as a result, 
- make data-informed decisions and 
- build customer experiences powered by data.

- How to / by what principles ? 
- the trifecta of data democratization
- Empower employees to feel comfortable asking data-related questions
- Provide the right tools to enable everybody to work with data
- Perceive democratization of data as an ongoing process which might even require an organization-wide cultural shift

- I have access to data but can’t trust the data
- The analytics tools my company provides aren’t designed for product teams
